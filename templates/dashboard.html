Cross-Sectional Momentum with Regime Filtering:
A Systematic Approach to U.S. Large-Cap Equity Selection
COMPASS v8.2 — Algorithm Design, Empirical Validation & Production Deployment
OmniCapital Research
February 2026 · Backtest Period: January 2000 – February 2026
Abstract

We present COMPASS v8.2, a systematic long-only equity strategy that combines cross-sectional momentum ranking with a macroeconomic regime filter and volatility-targeted position sizing. The algorithm selects the top 5 stocks by 90-day momentum (with 5-day reversal skip) from an annually-rotated S&P 500 universe of 40 stocks, rebalancing every 5 trading days. A regime filter (SPY vs. SMA-200, 3-day confirmation) reduces exposure to 2 positions during bear markets. Risk management includes individual position stops (−8%), trailing stops (+5%/−3%), and a portfolio circuit breaker (−15% drawdown). Over a 26-year backtest (January 2000–February 2026), the strategy achieves a gross CAGR of 18.56% (Sharpe 0.90, Max DD −26.9%) and a net CAGR of 15.16% (Sharpe 0.734, Max DD −30.3%) after 2.5% annual execution costs, growing $100,000 to $3.93M. The gross t-statistic of 3.74 exceeds the Harvey, Liu & Zhu (2016) threshold of 3.0 for multiple-testing adjustment. The algorithm was validated through 35 controlled experiments, of which 31 failed to improve upon baseline performance, confirming parameter inelasticity within this universe. A DSR p-value of ~0.30 indicates the Sharpe ratio is not statistically significant after full multiple-testing correction, warranting caution in interpreting point estimates as structural alpha.

1 Introduction

Cross-sectional momentum — buying recent winners and avoiding recent losers — is one of the most robust and well-documented anomalies in financial markets. Since Jegadeesh & Titman (1993) first demonstrated that stocks with high returns over the past 3–12 months tend to continue outperforming, momentum has been confirmed across asset classes, geographies, and time periods (Asness, Moskowitz & Pedersen, 2013). Carhart (1997) established momentum as a distinct risk factor (UMD), while Fama & French (2012) acknowledged its persistence in international equity markets.

However, raw momentum suffers from well-documented vulnerabilities: catastrophic crashes during market reversals (Daniel & Moskowitz, 2016), short-term reversal contamination (Novy-Marx, 2012), and high turnover that erodes returns after transaction costs (Frazzini, Israel & Moskowitz, 2015). COMPASS addresses each of these through a regime filter, reversal-adjusted signal, and concentrated 5-position portfolio with fixed 5-day hold periods.

The system evolved from OmniCapital v6, which exhibited a critical survivorship bias: a static 60-stock universe produced an illusory 16.92% CAGR that collapsed to 5.40% after correction with annual top-40 rotation. This failure motivated the development of COMPASS v8, which replaced static selection with a genuine momentum signal and introduced dynamic universe selection, regime filtering, and multi-layer risk management.

After 35 controlled experiments spanning 18 months of development, the algorithm has been declared inelastic: any modification to its core parameters degrades performance. This paper documents the complete algorithm design, provides a literature review situating COMPASS within the momentum research tradition, presents empirical results with cost analysis, catalogues all 37 experiments, and discusses statistical validity, limitations, and production deployment.

2 Literature Review
2.1 Momentum Foundations

Jegadeesh & Titman (1993) established the canonical momentum result: a long-short strategy buying 6-month winners and selling losers earns approximately 1% per month. This finding has been replicated by Fama & French (2012) across 23 developed markets and by Asness, Moskowitz & Pedersen (2013) across equities, bonds, currencies, and commodities. The behavioral explanations center on investor underreaction to information (Hong & Stein, 1999) and disposition effect selling by retail investors (Grinblatt & Keloharju, 2001).

2.2 Momentum Risk Management

Momentum strategies are vulnerable to sharp reversals, particularly during market stress. Daniel & Moskowitz (2016) documented that momentum crashes occur when high-beta losers rally violently during market recoveries. Barroso & Santa-Clara (2015) showed that scaling momentum exposure by the inverse of its realized volatility roughly doubles the Sharpe ratio, a finding that directly informs COMPASS’s volatility-targeting mechanism. The Faber (2007) trend-following filter — comparing price to a 10-month (approximately 200-day) moving average — provides an orthogonal risk reduction channel by reducing exposure during sustained bear markets.

2.3 Short-Term Reversal and Signal Design

Novy-Marx (2012) demonstrated that intermediate momentum (months 7–12) is more profitable than recent momentum (months 1–6) due to short-term reversal contamination. The standard practice of skipping the most recent month (or week) in momentum calculation follows from Jegadeesh & Titman (1993) and Lo & MacKinlay (1990). COMPASS implements a 5-day skip subtracted from the 90-day return, which functions as reversal adjustment rather than a simple exclusion period.

2.4 Multiple Testing and Backtest Overfitting

Harvey, Liu & Zhu (2016) demonstrated that the conventional t-statistic threshold of 2.0 is insufficient given the hundreds of factors tested in the literature, recommending a threshold of 3.0. Bailey, Borwein, López de Prado & Zhu (2014) introduced the Probability of Backtest Overfitting (PBO) via Combinatorially Symmetric Cross-Validation (CSCV), providing a framework for quantifying the likelihood that a backtest result is spurious. The Deflated Sharpe Ratio (DSR) from Bailey & López de Prado (2014) adjusts the observed Sharpe ratio for the number of trials, skewness, and kurtosis of returns.

2.5 Transaction Cost Impact

Frazzini, Israel & Moskowitz (2015) analyzed institutional execution costs for momentum strategies at AQR, finding median costs of 0.23% annually. Novy-Marx & Velikov (2016) showed that effective spread management and passive execution can preserve most of the gross momentum premium. COMPASS operates with conservative 2.5% annual cost assumption, which embeds substantial buffer above empirically observed institutional costs.

3 Methodology
3.1 Universe Selection

The investment universe consists of S&P 500 constituents, filtered annually on January 1 to the top 40 by dollar volume (Close × Volume), a proxy for market capitalization and liquidity. Stocks must have at least 63 trading days of price history. Over the 26-year backtest, 78 unique tickers appeared in the universe, with approximately 4–5 rotating each year. This dynamic annual rotation mitigates survivorship bias inherent in fixed-universe backtests, though residual bias remains because the S&P 500 constituent list from yfinance reflects current membership rather than historical point-in-time composition.

Residual survivorship bias from non-point-in-time S&P 500 membership is the primary data limitation. Norgate Data provides historical constituent lists and is the recommended next step (see Section 9).
3.2 Momentum Signal

The core signal is a reversal-adjusted 90-day cross-sectional momentum score. The 5-day skip is implemented as a subtraction (not an exclusion window), following the logic that short-term reversal is a contaminating signal that should be explicitly removed (Novy-Marx, 2012):

// Eq. 1: Reversal-adjusted momentum signal
momentum_90d = Price[t−5] / Price[t−95] − 1
reversal_5d  = Price[t] / Price[t−5] − 1
SCOREi      = momentum_90d − reversal_5d

// Eq. 2: Cross-sectional ranking and selection
ranki       = CrossSectionalRank(SCOREi)
SELECT       = Top N stocks by ranki
N = 5  // RISK_ON  (SPY > SMA200)
N = 2  // RISK_OFF (SPY < SMA200)

This reversal-adjusted formulation differs from the standard skip-month convention (Jegadeesh & Titman, 1993), where the most recent month is simply excluded from the lookback window. The subtraction approach actively penalizes stocks with strong recent reversals, providing additional crash protection during volatility spikes when short-term mean-reversion is strongest.

The 90-day lookback was validated through exhaustive parameter sweeps (60d, 90d, 120d, 180d). Ensemble averaging across multiple windows (60/90/120d) was tested as Experiment #23 and produced a catastrophic −7.43% CAGR degradation, confirming that signal dilution destroys alpha in concentrated portfolios.

3.3 Regime Filter

A macroeconomic regime filter governs market exposure. Inspired by Faber (2007)’s trend-following approach, the system compares SPY to its 200-day SMA. The 3-day confirmation requirement is a COMPASS-original design choice to reduce whipsaw from single-day crosses, not derived from Faber’s original specification:

// Eq. 3: Regime classification with 3-day confirmation
regime = RISK_ON  if SPY > SMA(200) for 3 consecutive days
regime = RISK_OFF if SPY < SMA(200) for 3 consecutive days

// 1,752 RISK_OFF days / 6,556 total = 26.7% protection time
The SMA-200 regime filter functions as a macroeconomic liquidity proxy, reflecting broad market conditions (credit spreads, earnings growth, monetary policy) rather than a tradeable microstructural inefficiency. This distinction matters: the signal cannot be arbitraged away because it captures economic regime changes, not pricing dislocations.
3.4 Position Sizing & Volatility Targeting

Following Barroso & Santa-Clara (2015), portfolio leverage is scaled inversely with realized volatility to maintain approximately constant risk exposure through time. The volatility target is set using SPY’s 20-day annualized volatility as the risk proxy, rather than portfolio-level volatility:

// Eq. 4: Volatility-targeted leverage
σrealized = Annualized 20-day volatility of SPY
leverage     = σtarget (15%) / σrealized
leverage     = clamp(leverage, 0.3, 1.0)

// Uses SPY vol as market risk proxy (not portfolio vol)
// Floor 0.3x prevents near-zero exposure during crises
// Ceiling 1.0x eliminates margin costs (no leverage)
The choice of SPY volatility over portfolio volatility is intentional: with only 5 concentrated positions rotating every 5 days, portfolio-level volatility is noisy and non-stationary. SPY provides a stable, forward-looking risk estimate that avoids look-ahead bias in position sizing. This differs from Barroso & Santa-Clara (2015), who use the momentum strategy’s own historical volatility.

3.6 Recovery Logic (Post-Circuit Breaker)

After a −15% portfolio stop, the system enters a staged recovery protocol requiring RISK-ON confirmation at each stage. The portfolio stop triggered 11 times across 26 years, generating approximately 2,002 protection days (30.5% of the backtest including recovery stages):

// Eq. 5: Staged recovery protocol

Stage 2:  Days 63–126 → 1.0x leverage, max 3 positions

// Requires RISK_ON to advance stages
// 11 circuit breaker events in 26 years
// 1,752 RISK_OFF days + 250 recovery overlap = ~2,002 total protection days
3.7 Cash Yield

Cash balances (including protection mode periods) earn the Moody’s Aaa seasoned corporate bond yield, sourced from FRED (series AAA). This represents the theoretical yield on a portfolio of highly-rated investment-grade corporate bonds and serves as an upper bound on achievable cash returns. In practice, money market funds or short-term Treasury instruments would yield 50–100bps less. The average yield over 2000–2025 is approximately 4.8%, with historical range of 2.6%–7.5%.

The Moody’s Aaa yield overstates achievable cash returns by ~50–100bps relative to money market funds. This optimistic assumption partially offsets the conservative 2.5% execution cost assumption, but users should model their actual cash vehicle yield for precise net returns.

4.3 Cost Analysis

Execution costs are modeled using two approaches: a granular decomposition and a simplified fixed-cost model. The production system uses the 2.5% fixed cost for conservatism:

// Eq. 6: Granular cost decomposition

// Eq. 7: Simplified production model
Net CAGR = Signal 18.56% − 2.5% fixed = 15.16%
// 2.5% is conservative; benchmarks: AQR 0.23%, retail 0.80%
The 2.5% fixed cost assumption is intentionally conservative (roughly 2x typical institutional costs) to provide margin of safety. The −2.92% MOC gap in the granular model reflects the difference between signal-time prices (Close[T−1]) and actual execution prices (Close[T]), which is a timing cost, not a transaction cost per se. With pre-close signal computation at 15:30 ET and same-day MOC execution, +0.79% of this gap is recovered.
4.4 Pre-Close Execution Advantage

Computing the signal at 15:30 ET using Close[T−1] prices and executing same-day MOC (Market-on-Close) orders recovers +0.79% CAGR and improves Max DD by 7.8 percentage points versus next-day execution. Ranking agreement between Close[T−1] and Close[T] is high (Spearman ρ = 0.951), making the slight signal staleness acceptable for a 5-day holding period.

4.5 Protection Mode Analysis

The −15% portfolio stop triggered 11 times across 26 years, generating approximately 2,002 protection days (30.5% of the backtest including recovery stages). Of these, 8 of 11 protection periods were net profitable due to cash yield during recovery. All attempts to improve protection mode performance — inverse ETFs (SH/SDS), momentum-based shorts, dynamic VIX/breadth triggers, TLT/IEF bond hedges — failed to outperform cash. The 2022 bond-equity correlation breakdown definitively rejected the last remaining hedge candidate.

7.2 Methodological Notes on Statistical Tests

PBO (25–35%): This is an analytical estimate, not computed via the full Combinatorially Symmetric Cross-Validation (CSCV) procedure of Bailey et al. (2014). A rigorous PBO implementation requires partitioning the backtest into S sub-samples and testing all C(S, S/2) combinations, which has not been performed. The reported range should be treated as indicative.

DSR p-value (~0.30): Computed using N=35 trials, observed Sharpe 0.90, estimated Sharpe skewness and kurtosis from the return series. The DSR inputs are: SR̂ = 0.90, N = 35, T = 6,556 days, skew(SR) ≈ −0.4, kurt(SR) ≈ 4.2. The p-value of 0.30 means we cannot reject the null hypothesis that the observed Sharpe is due to multiple testing at any conventional significance level. This is the most sobering statistical result and should weight heavily in position sizing decisions.

Survivorship bias (−1.5% to −4.0%): The expanded range follows Elton, Gruber & Blake (1996), who estimated survivorship bias of 0.9% annually for equity mutual funds. For a concentrated momentum strategy selecting from a survivorship-biased universe, the impact is amplified: delisted losers that would have been selected are absent from the backtest. The lower bound (−1.5%) assumes most eliminated stocks were already below top-40 by dollar volume; the upper bound (−4.0%) assumes several would have entered the momentum ranking and triggered stop losses.

7.4 Known Risks

Tax drag: ~209 trades/year generates short-term capital gains (up to 37% federal + state tax). After-tax returns in a taxable account fall to approximately 7–12% CAGR depending on tax bracket and state. The recommended mitigation is trading exclusively in tax-advantaged accounts (IRA/401(k)/Roth).

Crisis correlation: During flash crashes, correlations approach 1.0 and overnight gaps can bypass the −15% portfolio stop. This is inherent to concentrated long-only strategies. The May 2010 flash crash and March 2020 COVID selloff are historical examples where intraday stops would not have fired in time.

Regime dependence: The algorithm’s alpha is concentrated in RISK-ON periods. During the 30.5% of time spent in protection/recovery mode, returns are limited to cash yield. A prolonged bear market (>2 years) would significantly reduce compound returns relative to point estimates.

Alpha decay: Sub-period analysis (Table 3) shows declining excess returns from +27% (2000–2002) to +5.1% (2023–2026). McLean & Pontiff (2016) found that published anomalies lose approximately 32% of their returns post-publication. Cross-sectional momentum, first published in 1993, has had 30+ years of institutional adoption, and strategy crowding may continue to compress returns.

10 Discussion
10.1 Why Does COMPASS Work?

COMPASS exploits three well-documented behavioral and structural phenomena in U.S. large-cap equities: (1) investor underreaction to firm-specific news, which creates serial correlation in returns over 1–12 month horizons (Hong & Stein, 1999); (2) disposition effect selling by retail investors, who sell winners too early and hold losers too long, creating predictable price patterns (Grinblatt & Keloharju, 2001); and (3) institutional herding, where large fund flows amplify momentum in the direction of recent performance (Sias, 2004).

The regime filter adds value by avoiding the well-documented momentum crash phenomenon (Daniel & Moskowitz, 2016): when markets reverse sharply from bear to bull, momentum portfolios that are long defensive/low-beta stocks and short high-beta stocks suffer catastrophic losses. COMPASS sidesteps this by going flat during RISK-OFF periods, sacrificing 26.7% of trading time for substantially reduced tail risk.

10.2 Algorithm Inelasticity

The term “inelastic” describes COMPASS’s extreme sensitivity to parameter changes: 31 of 35 modifications degraded performance. This is not proof of genuine optimality — it may indicate overfitting to the specific 2000–2026 sample path. However, several observations support the structural interpretation: (a) the 90-day lookback aligns with the peak of the documented momentum premium at 3–12 months; (b) the 5-day hold captures the alpha window before mean-reversion dominates; (c) the −15% stop triggers only in genuine crises, not during normal volatility; (d) the SMA-200 regime filter reflects a consensus macroeconomic indicator used by institutional managers globally.

The strongest counter-argument is the DSR p-value of 0.30: after adjusting for 35 trials, we cannot reject the null hypothesis that the observed Sharpe is random. This tension between strong absolute performance and weak multiple-testing significance is unresolved and represents the central uncertainty of the strategy.

10.3 Alpha Decay Risk

McLean & Pontiff (2016) found that anomaly returns decline by ~32% post-publication. Cross-sectional momentum was published in 1993, giving 30+ years of institutional adoption. The sub-period analysis (Table 3) shows excess returns declining from +27% to +5.1%, consistent with gradual crowding. If this trend continues linearly, excess returns could compress to zero by the mid-2030s. However, the behavioral roots of momentum (disposition effect, underreaction) are structural to human psychology and unlikely to be fully arbitraged away.

10.4 Capacity Constraints

At $100K initial capital, COMPASS trades highly liquid S&P 500 names with negligible market impact. The strategy’s capacity limit is estimated at $5–10M before MOC slippage begins to degrade performance, based on typical large-cap MOC volumes of $50–200M per name. Beyond $10M, passive limit execution within the MOC window or TWAP algorithms would be necessary to manage market impact.

11 Conclusion

COMPASS v8.2 achieves a net CAGR of 15.16% (Sharpe 0.734) after conservative 2.5% annual execution costs over a 26-year backtest, outperforming SPY buy-and-hold by approximately +7% annually with roughly half the maximum drawdown. The gross t-statistic of 3.74 clears the Harvey, Liu & Zhu (2016) threshold for newly discovered factors, and alpha is positive across all sub-periods.

However, several material caveats temper this result: (1) the DSR p-value of ~0.30 indicates the Sharpe ratio is not statistically significant after adjusting for 37 experiments; (2) residual survivorship bias from non-point-in-time S&P 500 data may overstate returns by 1.5–4.0%; (3) sub-period excess returns show a declining trend consistent with alpha decay; (4) the Moody’s Aaa cash yield overstates achievable cash returns by ~50–100bps.

After all statistical adjustments, we estimate a true expected net CAGR of 10–13% with a 90% confidence interval of 6.5–15.5%. Even at the lower bound, this represents meaningful alpha over passive indexing. The recommended path forward is: (1) Norgate Data for point-in-time validation, (2) IBKR paper trading for 3–6 months, and (3) live deployment in a tax-advantaged account at $100K initial capital.

The algorithm is declared locked: no further motor experiments will be conducted. All future improvements must be chassis-level (execution, data quality, infrastructure) rather than signal-level modifications.
